What is agentic AI?

Generative AI creates content and answers questions. You ask, it responds. It's the kind of AI you've been using in chat tools for the last few years.

Agentic AI takes actions across your systems — email, tickets, calendars, files, workflows. You give it a job, and it carries out the steps. It can move, send, update, route, and execute. That's a step-change in leverage. It's also a step-change in risk.

The shift, in one line
Generative AI changed how we think.
Agentic AI changes how work gets done.

Why this matters for your business

A small team with an agentic agent doesn't get more answers. It gets more done. That's the leverage. The downside is that an agent acting on your behalf creates more surface area for mistakes — wrong recipients, wrong updates, wrong actions taken in your name. The rest of this guide is about capturing the leverage and avoiding that downside.

02 · Readiness Checklist
The readiness checklist

Before you build, check these 10 things.

Three categories, mapped to the framework. If you can't tick a box yet, that's where to focus first.

FoundationBrief
01You have one specific task in mind — not a category, a single task.
“Automate our sales process” is a category. “Draft a follow-up email after every discovery call” is a task.
02The task is repetitive and follows a predictable pattern.
If the steps change every time, the agent has nothing to learn from.
03The data the agent needs is clean, organized, and accessible.
Garbage in, faster garbage out. Fix the data first.
Ownership & SuccessBrief
04You have a named human owner for the agent.
Someone who watches it, fixes it, and is on the hook when it goes wrong.
05You can describe success in one sentence.
If success is vague, the agent will be vague.
06You can describe failure in one sentence.
Knowing what “going wrong” looks like is how you catch it early.
Guardrails & OversightLaneLoop
07You've defined what the agent IS allowed to do.
Explicit allow lists are clearer than “use good judgment.”
08You've defined what the agent is NOT allowed to do.
Spell out the hard nos — don't assume they're obvious.
09You have a human review step in the workflow.
Especially for outbound actions: emails sent, records updated, money moved.
10You have an escalation path when things go wrong.
Who gets the alert, how fast, and what they're empowered to do about it.

03 · Setup Walkthrough
The setup walkthrough

From idea to working agent.

Seven concrete steps. Run them in order. You can do the first three with a notebook — no software required.

Brief Steps 1–3 — define the one job.
1
Document the task as it works today

What to do — Write down the current process step by step: who does it, what triggers it, what they produce, and where it commonly breaks.

Why it matters — You can't automate what you can't describe. This step alone often reveals the task isn't well-defined enough yet.

2
Define inputs and outputs explicitly

What to do — List every input the agent needs (data, context, instructions, access) and every output it must produce (format, destination, frequency).

Why it matters — Most early agent failures aren't intelligence problems. They're interface problems.

3
Write the one-sentence brief

What to do — Complete this sentence: “This agent's only job is to ________.” If you can't fit it in one sentence, the scope is too big.

Why it matters — Vagueness is the number-one reason early agent projects fail. Specificity is your friend.

Lane Step 4 — set the boundaries before it acts.
4
Set the lane — allow and deny lists

What to do — Make two short lists. What the agent IS allowed to do. What it is NOT allowed to do. Be specific. Include actions, systems, recipients, and topics.

Why it matters — AI defaults to helpful. Without explicit limits, helpful becomes harmful.

03 · Setup Walkthrough · continued
Steps 5–7

Once it works, prove it works.

The last three steps are where most teams skip ahead and regret it. Don't.

Loop Steps 5–7 — keep a human in control.
5
Build the human checkpoint

What to do — Decide who reviews, when they review, and what authority they have to stop, change, or approve the output.

Why it matters — Trust is built one verified output at a time. Don't go fully autonomous before you've earned the right to.

6
Run a parallel trial

What to do — For two to four weeks, let the agent produce alongside the existing human process. Compare outputs side by side and log every disagreement.

Why it matters — You learn where the agent fails before it fails at scale. The trial is where the real design work happens.

7
Define your kill switch

What to do — Decide in advance what triggers shutting it down — and who has the authority to pull the trigger. Document both.

Why it matters — When something goes wrong, you don't want to be making policy under pressure.

04 · Common Pitfalls
Common pitfalls

Five mistakes to skip.

Every one of these shows up in our work with clients. Catching them now is much cheaper than catching them later.

✕
Going too big, too fast

“Let's automate the entire department.” Don't. Pick one boring, valuable, repeatable task and build that one well. Earn the right to scope up.

✕
Skipping the human review

Trust is earned, not granted. Build the review step in from day one and remove it gradually — not the other way around.

✕
Pointing the agent at messy data

The agent inherits your data quality issues and then amplifies them at speed. Spend a week on data hygiene before you spend a week on agent design.

✕
Ignoring prompt injection

Inputs (documents, emails, web pages) can contain hidden instructions that an agent will follow. Assume hostile content exists, and limit what your agent can do with information from outside sources.

✕
No defined success metric

If you can't measure it, you can't manage it. Pick one number that defines success and one that defines failure — and watch both.
