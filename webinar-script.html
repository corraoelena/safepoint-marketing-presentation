3. The Content — The 3 Secrets

20:00–55:00

Each secret breaks ONE false belief. Teach real value in each — but every secret should leave the audience more convinced of the Big Domino.

Secret #1 — “AI and the cloud aren't safe for a practice like ours.”

Breaks the: VEHICLE / opportunity false belief — that secure AI & cloud adoption simply doesn't work for healthcare.
Secret 1 title slide
It was never the tool that caused the breach. In almost every case I've seen, the tool was fine — the foundation was missing. Risk doesn't live in ‘AI’ or ‘the cloud.’ It lives in five gaps: no Business Associate Agreement, no idea where data goes, no access controls, no logging, and no one accountable for the vendor.

Teach this — the 5-gap checklist:

  • BAA — is there a signed Business Associate Agreement with every vendor touching PHI?
  • Data flow — do you know where patient data is stored, processed, and whether it trains a model?
  • Access — is access role-based, with MFA, and revoked when staff leave?
  • Visibility — is activity logged so you'd actually notice misuse?
  • Ownership — is one accountable party reviewing each tool, not “whoever signed up”?
Close those five and a cloud EHR or an AI scribe is demonstrably safer than the paper-and-email workflow it replaces — because now it's monitored, encrypted, and accountable. The status quo is the risky option.
Trial close: “How many of you have signed up for a tool without checking even one of these five? You're not careless — nobody handed you the checklist. Now you have it.”

Secret #2 — “We're too small and don't have the expertise.”

Breaks the: INTERNAL false belief — that THEY personally can't pull it off, even if the method works.
Secret 2 title slide
You do not need to become a security expert, and you do not need to hire a CISO. You need a foundation that's set up once and then maintained for you. The practices doing this well aren't the ones with big IT teams — they're the ones who stopped trying to do it alone.

Teach this — the co-managed model:

  • Set-up-once foundation: assessment, BAAs, access controls and logging configured a single time, not reinvented per tool.
  • A simple “can we adopt this?” decision flow your front desk can actually follow.
  • A co-managed partner handles the security plumbing; your team keeps owning patient care.
  • Staff awareness training so the humans — the #1 breach vector — become a strength.
Small is actually an advantage here. Fewer systems, fewer vendors, and you can put a clean foundation in place in weeks, not years. Size isn't your obstacle — going it alone is.
Trial close: “If the security part was handled for you, and your team just had a simple yes/no checklist — that's doable for a practice your size, right?”

Secret #3 — “It's too expensive and it'll disrupt our clinicians.”

Breaks the: EXTERNAL false belief — outside obstacles (cost, time, disruption to care) they believe will stop them.
Secret 3 title slide
The expensive option is the breach — $7.42 million on average, and 279 days of your team's life spent cleaning it up. A security foundation is a rounding error next to that. And done right, it speeds clinicians up, because the safe path becomes the easy path instead of a series of nerve-wracking guesses.

Teach this — cost & disruption, reframed:

  • Cost of doing it right: a fixed, predictable monthly figure — known in advance, budgetable.
  • Cost of a breach: direct fines, lost patients, 279 days of disruption, reputational damage.
  • Disruption myth: a phased rollout means clinicians never lose a tool they rely on; they gain approved ones faster.
  • Speed dividend: when “is this allowed?” has a 30-second answer, adoption accelerates instead of stalling in fear.
So the real question isn't ‘can we afford to do this?’ It's ‘can we afford to keep adopting tools without it?’
Trial close: “When you compare a predictable monthly cost to a seven-figure breach — which one actually sounds expensive?”
